# First AI Game in 10 Minutes

A no-coding-required guide for middle school teachers.

---

## What this is

In 10 minutes of class time, you and your students will tell an AI to build a working video game and play it together. Nobody needs to install anything. Nobody needs to know how to code. You just need a browser and a projector.

---

## Before class (one-time setup, ~5 minutes)

1. Go to **claude.ai** in any modern browser (Chrome, Edge, Safari, Firefox).
2. Click **Sign up**. Use your school email or a personal one. Verify the email.
3. You're in. The free plan is plenty for this exercise.
4. **Test it works**: in the chat box, type the starter prompt from the next section and confirm the game appears in the right-hand panel. (Some school networks block AI tools, so test before class.)

---

## The 10-minute classroom flow

**Setup (1 min)**
Open claude.ai on the projector. Tell the class: "We're going to ask an AI to build us a game. We tell it what we want in plain English. Let's see what it makes."

**Step 1: The first prompt (2 min)**
Paste this in the chat:

> Build me a single-page Snake game in HTML, CSS, and JavaScript. Make it colorful, with a score in the top corner and arrow-key controls. Keep the code in one file.

After ~30 seconds an **Artifact** appears in a panel on the right with the playable game.

**Step 2: Play it as a class (3 min)**
Let a student come up and play. Try to crash on purpose. Notice what works, what doesn't, what looks weird.

**Step 3: Iterate by asking (3 min)**
Ask the class: "What should we change?" Take suggestions and type them in as new messages. Examples that work well:

- "Make the snake purple and the apple yellow."
- "Add a 'Game Over' screen when you hit yourself."
- "Make it speed up every 5 points."
- "Add sound effects when you eat an apple."

The Artifact updates in place. Each change is its own version, and you can scroll back through them with the arrows at the top.

**Step 4: Share with the class (1 min)**
Click the **Share** button at the top of the Artifact, then **Publish to web**. You get a link any student can open on their own device. The game stays live as long as you want it to.

## Other game prompts to try

Mix it up across class sessions:

- "Build me a single-page memory matching game with 12 animal emoji cards."
- "Build me a simple typing speed test where words fall from the top of the screen."
- "Build me a single-page math quiz game that gives me a multiplication problem and tracks my score."
- "Build me a single-page asteroid-dodger game where I move with arrow keys."

---

## Tips for getting good results

- **Be specific.** "Make a game" gives you something random. "Build a single-page Snake game in HTML with arrow keys and a score" gives you what you want.
- **Always say "single page" or "one file."** Otherwise it may split the code across files and the Artifact won't render.
- **If something breaks, just say so.** "The snake doesn't appear on screen. Fix that." It will try again.
- **Don't worry about reading the code.** The Artifact panel renders the game. The code is for the curious; the goal is the game.

## Discussion questions for after

Bring the magic back to learning:

- What surprised you? What did the AI do that you didn't expect?
- What did the AI get wrong on the first try? How did we fix it?
- Where did you have to be very specific in your wording?
- If you could ask the AI to build any game, what would you ask for, and how would you describe it?

These conversations are where the real learning happens. The AI is the toy. Thinking clearly about what you want is the skill.

---

## What's next?

If a student gets hooked and wants to keep the game forever (not just as a shared link), the next step is publishing it as their own website. The simplest free option:

1. In the Artifact, click the **Copy code** button.
2. Save the code as `index.html` on your computer.
3. Go to **netlify.com/drop** and drag the file onto the page.
4. You get a real public URL in 10 seconds.

That's a great Day 2 extension activity.
